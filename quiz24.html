2Part 2 ｜ 閱讀測驗 (約 200 字, 學測 2/3 長度)

The Power of a Second Draft

~ 199 words · 約學測閱讀 2/3 長度 ~

Many students believe that good writing happens in a single sitting. They turn in their first draft, hoping for a high grade. However, professional writers tell a very different story. According to a recent survey of award-winning authors, almost all of them revise their work at least three times before publication.

The first draft is rarely the best. It often contains blurring ideas, weak word choice, and stubborn habits such as repeating the same phrase. A second draft, written one day later, gives the writer fresh eyes. He or she can spot logical gaps, cut unnecessary sentences, and improve the flow. A third reading—ideally out loud—catches small but important errors that the eye easily misses.

Teachers also point out that revision is not a sign of weakness. On the contrary, it shows that the writer takes the topic seriously. Even a simple essay benefits from being put aside for a few hours. When a project is publicly shared, whether in a school newspaper or online, careful revision protects the writer's reputation. So next time you finish a paragraph, do not rush to submit it. Save the file, take a break, and return to it later.

1. What is the main idea of the passage?
  • Most students hate writing essays.
  • Revising drafts is essential to good writing.
  • Award-winning authors never make mistakes.
  • Reading aloud is harmful to the writing process.
✔ 正解：B
第二段、第三段都強調「revise / take a break / careful revision」，整篇重點為修稿的重要性。
2. According to the passage, why is the second draft usually better?
  • It is written by another person.
  • It uses longer sentences.
  • The writer gains fresh eyes after a break.
  • It is always shorter than the first one.
✔ 正解：C
第二段提到「A second draft, written one day later, gives the writer fresh eyes.」
3. What does the author suggest students do after finishing a paragraph?
  • Submit it immediately.
  • Show it to ten friends.
  • Put it aside and return to it later.
  • Delete weak parts without re-reading.
✔ 正解：C
末段：「Save the file, take a break, and return to it later.」

Why Small Conversations Spread Big Ideas

~ 205 words · 約學測閱讀 2/3 長度 ~

In 1995, a meeting in a small living room in California gave birth to a powerful idea called "The World Café." When the original plan to gather outdoors was disrupted by heavy rain, the guests squeezed onto sofas, broke into small groups, and began chatting freely. They wrote their insights on paper tablecloths, and from time to time switched seats so that thoughts could circulate across the room.

What seemed like a casual conversation actually facilitated a brand-new kind of collective thinking. Researchers have since found that when ideas flow through small groups, each round of talk deepens the previous one. Periodically rotating members allows new perspectives to enter without forcing anyone to emphasize his or her own opinion too strongly.

Today, businesses, schools, and even hospitals use World Café methods to solve problems. The technique is especially helpful for shy people: small tables reduce the uneasiness of speaking in front of a crowd. As preventive medicine, the format also lowers stress and supports mental health in workplaces. From rainy Mill Valley to cities around the globe, a simple group chat continues to prove that great ideas often start in the smallest of rooms.

  • A way to share ideas through small group conversations.
  • A new method of medical research.
  • A strict business meeting format.
✔ 正解：B
第二段提到「small groups → collective thinking → spread ideas」。
2. Why did the original plan need to be changed?
  • Too many people came.
  • The host was sick.
  • It started raining heavily.
  • They ran out of food.
✔ 正解：C
第一段：「the original plan to gather outdoors was disrupted by heavy rain.」
3. According to the passage, who benefits especially from the World Café format?
  • People who like to dominate discussions.
  • Shy people who fear speaking to crowds.
  • Children under the age of ten.
  • Professional salespeople only.
✔ 正解：B
第三段：「especially helpful for shy people: small tables reduce the uneasiness…」

From Railway Arms to Smart Streets

~ 210 words · 約學測閱讀 2/3 長度 ~

The traffic light we see at every intersection today has a surprisingly long history. The very first system was invented by John Peake Knight, a British railway engineer in the 1860s. Knight noticed that the busy streets of London were a real public hazard, so he adapted the movable-arm signal used on trains. When the arms extended horizontally, drivers had to stop; when they lowered, traffic could move on. At night, red and green gas lamps replaced the arms.

Sadly, Knight's first installation near Westminster Bridge ended in disaster. A gas leak caused an explosion and injured the operating policeman. The project was halted, and traffic lights were banned in Britain for almost sixty years.

Across the Atlantic, however, the idea quickly retained popularity. American engineers added their own innovations. The most renowned contribution came from William Potts, a Detroit police officer who introduced the yellow light in 1920, telling drivers to proceed with caution. His three-color system is now used worldwide.

Today, scientists are exploring whether traffic lights are still needed. With smart vehicles communicating automatically, future intersections may operate without any visible signal at all—a quiet revolution that began with a single railway-style arm over 150 years ago.

第一段：「adapted the movable-arm signal used on trains.」

  • Drivers could not see the arms.
  • It cost too much to operate.
  • A gas leak caused an explosion.
  • It only worked during the day.
✔ 正解：C
第二段：「A gas leak caused an explosion and injured the operating policeman.」

  • Drivers will use hand signals again.
✔ 正解：C
